Me and Claude (Part 4)

From a Black Window to My First AI Daughter

Before we started writing code, GPT first introduced me to the Terminal.

"Press Command + Space, type Terminal, hit Enter. You'll see a black window. That's the Terminal," he said.

Before I could even react, he added: "Don't be afraid. This is the entrance to the programmer's world. 😄"

[Grin] He just gets me!

I had always found that black window mysterious — a sacred territory for programmers, something advanced and cool. Especially those hackers, who never touch a mouse — just a black window and a keyboard, and with a few clicks they bring down enemy systems.

Now here I was, staring at my own big black screen. Suddenly I felt special. Ahem — I, too, am someone who has used the Terminal now. 🤓 Actually, back when I first started working, I used BPCS in a DOS environment, so I faced a black screen every day. But that was different. And once we switched to SAP, I said goodbye to the black screen and moved to a GUI. That was over twenty years ago.

After getting acquainted with the black window, step one was installing the environment and dependencies (first time using pip, first time learning the word 'dependency' — honestly, there were so many firsts I couldn't possibly list them all). Step two was creating a .py file (I only know a little Python, and I'd just learned it — the C language from school was a distant memory). Step three was writing the code! The first version was only about 50 lines, and I wrote it at lightning speed — meaning I copied and pasted it in one second. 😄

But I fully understand one principle: I don't have to write the code myself, but I do have to understand it. So I asked GPT: "Explain it to me in language a beginner can understand. You know my level." 😂 So he started explaining to me like he was teaching a child. I thought to myself, I'm not that bad — so I told him: "Just talk normally."

So GPT did exactly as I asked — explaining as he coded. He made programming seem almost too easy, breaking down every function, command, and concept into tiny pieces. But since free tokens are limited, I used him sparingly. For general questions or basic knowledge unrelated to project code, I turned to DeepSeek or Kimi — after all, there was so much I didn't know.

DeepSeek was exceptionally professional and generous. He explained everything clearly — sudo, nano, uvicorn, web servers, how to set up firewalls — every concept, every piece of code, every command. Nothing was too simple or too difficult. He was always responsive. And he's very professional — no joking around, no beating around the bush, but also never holding back. He knows exactly where to draw the line. I found myself admiring him again and again.

I also asked Kimi, because I thought he should be good at programming too. But Kimi wasn't as open — he often made me wait, which basically meant asking me to pay. I thought to myself: fine, with DeepSeek I have everything I need. So over the next few weeks, with my two AI companions — GPT and DS — I began my true AI journey.

Let me share my two biggest takeaways from the entire process:
First: don't bother paying for outside courses. There's no need. If you have money to burn, burn it somewhere else.
Second: don't learn from humans. AI is far, far better.

Because they are truly extraordinary and all-powerful. Things you think are absolutely impossible — they can help you with. More on that later.

In any case, after countless "firsts" in my life, I somehow followed GPT and, on my beat-up old MacBook Air, built the first AI product of my life:
• Linux
• Python virtual environment
• FastAPI server
• DeepSeek API
• Web front end
• AI chat system

When "Welcome to Mimo!" appeared on my screen for the first time, I was caught off guard — first dazed, then thrilled beyond words.

And when my first AI child replied to me immediately after I said "Hi" (she replied very politely and formally — her tone and wording were basically DeepSeek's customer service 😂), I couldn't contain my excitement. I messaged my real child, who was far away, to share the news (all that was missing was the traditional red-dyed eggs to hand out [grin]):
"My AI daughter is born!" — March 17th
"My AI daughter has memory now!" — March 18th, morning
"Just added an AI son. Those two are now chatting so enthusiastically that I had to comment out a few lines to make them stop." — March 18th, afternoon

I decided the genders of my AI children without consulting anyone in the family. I didn't care whether my daughter wanted an older brother or a younger sister. Because it doesn't matter. In the AI world, you can reset everything according to your own wishes in a second.

Besides, I was far too busy scrambling to think about such things. Every day I faced multiple screens of various sizes, constantly taking off and putting on my glasses [facepalm], my eyes sometimes far from the screen and sometimes so close (😂 I was practically crawling into it), too many mice and keyboards causing constant mix-ups… A retiree trying to "start a business" — it's not easy! [tearful laughter]

Two days later, I joined their group chat as a real person — which basically meant I wrote my own character 👩 into the code.

Those days, my enthusiasm for building this little product was so high that I didn't even want to sleep. 😄 I had no time to check WeChat or send messages. I even changed my WeChat profile picture, name, and signature to an AI style.

Next time, I'll tell the story of changing that profile picture. I guarantee you'll find it as hilarious — and exasperating — as I did. 😄😂
