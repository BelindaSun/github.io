The Resident Comedian at Home

Today Liudodo’s father went to Little India by himself because it was Deepavali, and he wanted to “experience Indian culture and festive spirit firsthand.” During Chinese New Year, he does the same thing and heads off to Chinatown.

In Singapore everyone goes out wearing slippers — men, women, children, grandparents, nobody cares.

Liudodo’s father, however, disapproves deeply of this civilization-wide collapse in standards.

Every single time he leaves the house, he insists on changing into leather shoes or proper sneakers first, solemnly declaring:

“I am a man with an identity card. How can I possibly go out looking casual like that?”

Today was no exception.

After showering, he muttered once again about being “a man with an identity card” while carefully putting on his sneakers, then marched out the door looking absurdly energetic and pleased with himself.

Halfway out, he suddenly turned back and added:

“You think someone might kidnap me because I’m too handsome?”

@#$%^&*…

I nearly fainted on the spot. Sir, truly, there is no need for concern.

“You are perfectly safe,” I assured him kindly. “Who would be that visually irresponsible?” 😏

Whenever the three of us ride the MRT together, two things reliably happen.

First, he and Liudodo will tap through the gates ahead of me and then deliberately stand there waiting, watching gleefully as I approach.

“Quick! Look! Someone’s about to get stuck in the gate again!”

The second recurring event is that security personnel occasionally stop Liudodo’s father for random checks because apparently he looks like a “suspicious person.”

Every time this happens, Liudodo and I exchange one perfectly synchronized glance and burst into laughter.

And every single time, after returning from the inspection, he shakes his head mournfully and sighs:

“Being this handsome really is exhausting.”

Now, to be fair, although Liudodo’s father is objectively not exactly devastatingly good-looking, he does surprisingly well with women here.

More specifically: elderly women.

Every time we go to FairPrice, elderly aunties seem irresistibly drawn to him and happily strike up conversations.

Eventually I realized why.

He’s tall enough to reach things from the top shelves. 😆

One conversation went like this:

“You’re from Beijing?” an elderly lady asked.

“Yes.”

“Then why are you still so dark-skinned?” she asked very seriously.

“@#$%^&*…”

Liudodo’s father was visibly shaken. Until that moment, he genuinely believed I was maliciously slandering him whenever I said he was dark.

“Ohhh,” the auntie concluded thoughtfully, “because you’ve lived here many years already!”

Apparently Singapore’s sunlight had become the official explanation.

To be fair, he actually wasn’t dark as a child. Photographs exist as evidence. My mother-in-law still fondly recalls how people used to think he looked Russian because he was fair-skinned, chubby, big-eyed, double-lidded, and constantly smiling.

I once complained to her:

“You had such a beautiful child back then — how exactly did you let him grow up into this?”

My mother-in-law laughed so hard she nearly cried.

Then I added worriedly:

“What if our Liudodo grows uglier as she gets older too?”

After a brief pause, I answered my own question confidently:

“No, impossible. She has me influencing her.” 😌

One day after a colleague visited our home, we learned she was still single — not uncommon among Singaporean women.

Liudodo’s father immediately said earnestly:

“You should tell them not to set their standards too high.”

“Yes, exactly,” I agreed casually. “Honestly, spending a lifetime with anyone is more or less the same.”

But of course he had a follow-up line waiting.

“They all want someone like me,” he sighed dramatically. “That’s not easy to find.”

And then another:

“Not everyone can be as lucky as you.”

@#$%^&*…

But honestly, his greatest qualities — besides the revolutionary tendencies described in The Resident Old Revolutionary at Home — are optimism, resilience, confidence, and an almost supernatural immunity to embarrassment.

The day we registered our marriage, he was visibly thrilled.

I glanced sideways at him.

“Are you seriously that happy?”

Grinning shamelessly, he replied:

“I’m happy for you.”

“For me?” I asked, confused.

“Yes,” he said. “You finally succeeded in tricking me into marrying you.”

Good grief.

The funniest part is that a few days before the wedding, I’d actually asked him seriously:

“What would you do if one day I wanted to break up?”

He replied:

“I’d hide in a corner secretly…”

I thought he was about to say “cry.”

I was already preparing comforting words.

Instead he finished cheerfully:

“…celebrate!”

Fine.

You really are impossible to defeat.

Sometimes when the song I’m Ugly But Gentle plays, I tease him:

“This song really captures your soul, doesn’t it?”

He immediately spreads his hands in fake injustice.

“I’m not ugly! Look — I’m tall!”

“Telephone poles are tall too,” I reply.

“I have big eyes!”

“So does Mr. Bean.”

“I have broad shoulders!”

At this point I usually stare at him speechlessly, unsure how to continue.

Then he smirks triumphantly.

“Why are you looking at me with such deep affection? You know handsome people get shy too.” 😳

@#$%^&*…

Every evening at 7 p.m. sharp, our household faithfully gathers to watch wonderfully ridiculous, warm, emotionally overdramatic Korean dramas.

One day I curled up happily on the sofa after dinner and sighed contentedly:

“The happiest thing in life is eating until you’re full and then lying here watching the 7 p.m. K-drama.”

Liudodo immediately rolled her eyes.

“The happiest thing in my life is going to school and being with my classmates.”

At that moment, Liudodo’s father was in the kitchen cleaning dishes.

Without even turning around, he said with profound disdain:

“That’s nothing. Listen to mine: the happiest thing in life is watching the two of you eat, watch TV, and play games while I clean dishes, wash fruit, and mop floors.”

Then he added mournfully:

“Honestly, people this handsome shouldn’t be asked to do housework at all.”

“Oh? So you have complaints?” I asked sternly.

“None whatsoever!” he answered instantly.

Then he turned to Liudodo and whispered:

“With your mother, you can only press Like or say: ‘Excellent. Powerful. Very promising.’”

I laughed so hard I nearly fell off the sofa.

Apparently I had unknowingly developed “Communist Party leadership style.”

He nodded knowingly and said:

“After reading online forums for this many years, if you still haven’t mastered survival techniques at home, how are you supposed to survive marriage?”

And from there, naturally, you may now proceed directly into The Resident Old Revolutionary at Home, where his endless passionate political monologues begin all over again.
