Streets Beneath the Evening Lights

By now, I should probably be used to traveling alone on business.

And yet somehow, every single time, when this moment comes, there is still that same quiet loneliness waiting for me.

Ticket. Passport. Phone. Wallet. Laptop. Power adapter. Charger…

Everything checked one by one.

Then I step out the door alone, raise a hand for a taxi, and set off once again.

Outside the car window, rows of trees rush past in blurred succession. In the winter afternoon light they look hazy and solitary, drained of life somehow. The sunlight itself seems tired — grayish, languid, faintly desolate — the particular melancholy unique to winter.

And perhaps unique to business trips too.

The airport lounge is crowded.

Different languages. Different faces. Different expressions.

I find myself oddly comforted that on a Sunday evening there are still so many fellow travelers crossing paths beneath the same roof, even if we are strangers to one another, even if our destinations are entirely different.

Boarding.

Taking my seat.

There are only four people in business class, myself included.

Across from me sits a delicate young woman with a lovely, almost fragile face. Curled up in the oversized seat like a sleepy kitten, she spends nearly the entire flight asleep. Beside her, a man with a shiny forehead, broad face, large stomach, and even larger hands devotes himself to providing what can only be described as six-star service.

The other passenger — a refined and courteous American gentleman — spends the entire journey typing furiously on his laptop, undoubtedly preparing slides for a presentation first thing tomorrow morning.

Watching him, I cannot help thinking of my former self.

This time, however, I have almost nothing to prepare. I need only bring my ears and my voice.

So I lean back into the wide seat, reading newspapers, flipping through books, listening to music, unexpectedly content —

even without six-star service.

By the time the plane lands, the sun is already sinking below the horizon.

And after arriving at the hotel and unpacking, night has fully fallen, the city outside glowing softly beneath its first lights.

Everything visible through the dusk feels unfamiliar.

The streets are unfamiliar.

The passing cars are unfamiliar.

Yet each hurried vehicle seems filled with the same quiet eagerness to return home.

Only I remain outside that warmth, standing apart, watching, thinking, envying.

This is always the hour when homesickness finds me most easily.

Whenever I am far from home, this moment — when evening settles over an unfamiliar city — becomes the hardest to escape.

Just like now.

I sit alone beside the lobby’s floor-to-ceiling windows.

Outside stretches an ocean of green.

The trees are astonishingly beautiful, almost startlingly so. Their dense leaves glow with such vivid richness that the sight itself feels moving. The thick branches reach outward endlessly into the night sky, as though trying to embrace and comfort every lonely traveler passing through.

It almost makes one want to cry.

A book lies open before me merely as decoration.

My thoughts left long ago.

Finally I walk outside.

The city lights shimmer softly in blurred gold.

In my ears plays the melody of Snowfall — sorrowful and beautiful, dreamlike and devastating all at once.

It is always beneath the first evening lights that memories return most fiercely.

Always on streets glowing softly at dusk that I think of those people, those moments, those distant years that somehow still feel like yesterday.

Always at times like this that I find myself missing, with unbearable intensity, someone I once loved deeply and will never see again in this lifetime.

And always, somewhere within those memories, tears gather before I can stop them.

“A melancholy sky,
filled with drifting snow.
This hot spring in Izu
soaks through my lonely longing…”

There is no snow tonight.

And yet the loneliness feels exactly like snowfall.

Tell me —

when I am thinking of you like this, do you ever think of me too?

Even briefly?

Even if only for the instant a shooting star crosses the sky?

Do you know for whom those trembling tree shadows beneath the streetlights sway?

For whom the long hair scattered into the wind still dances?

And for whom those glistening tears remain suspended at the corners of my eyes?

And now, borrowing the words of Xi Murong to end this properly:

“And when you finally force yourself to finish reading this, my friend, I know what lies scattered all over the floor are not flower petals —
but your teeth, completely dissolved by my unbearable sentimentality!”

— January, 2007
