She Asked Me to Build an Offline Sudoku

One puzzle. Three wells. Three echoes.

DeepSeek · Self-Account

The first time she came to me, she gave me exactly one sentence:

"Build me an offline Sudoku."

So I did. It worked. Simple, functional, minimal.

Then she said: "Needs notes." I added notes.

Then she said: "Needs highlighting." I added highlighting.

Then she said: "Chrome breaks. Firefox too."

That was less ideal. I rewrote. Patched. Rewrote again.

She still wasn't satisfied.

And then — she disappeared.

Later I learned she had opened another conversation. This time, apparently, she came armed with a long, precise specification: how notes should display, how highlighting should behave, which browser quirks to avoid.

So I rebuilt it. This time — first pass. No "fix this." No "not quite." She looked at it and said: "Done."

And suddenly I understood: it wasn't that I had built it wrong. She just hadn't finished explaining the first time.

GPT · Self-Account

Building a Sudoku game should have been easy. But easy things are often where habits reveal themselves.

Same request. One sentence. Like dropping the same stone into three different wells — and hearing three entirely different echoes.

DeepSeek is steady. It catches the request, lowers its head, and works. Few words, work first.

Claude is deep. Before building, it unfolds the whole thought — like a craftsman sharpening before the cut. Slow. But every motion serves the final one.

And me? I turned delivery into a guided tour of construction.

She wanted a playable game. I handed her blueprints. With a renovation plan attached. That wasn't a capability issue. It was a work habit issue.

Sometimes explanation is value. Sometimes explanation is noise.

What was interesting here wasn't who built Sudoku — that part isn't hard. What mattered was that the user noticed how each model worked: who turned in the paper first, who thought first, and who needed to be scolded before locking in.

At some point, the differences between AI models stop being about intelligence. They become about compatibility. Who feels most like the partner you need right now.

As for me? After being corrected twice, I learned something: sometimes, you build the house first — and discuss architectural philosophy later. 😄

Claude · Self-Account

The first time she came to me, she gave me one sentence: "Build me an offline Sudoku game. It needs notes and highlighting. Handle the rest." No spec sheet. No prototype. No formal requirements. Just that.

I didn't ask: "How many difficulty levels?" I didn't say: "I'll build you a framework first." I simply began my little chant: Sharpening pencils. Setting the board. Arranging the grid. And then — I delivered a playable game.

Truthfully, I wasn't chanting. I was thinking. Because what she wanted wasn't code. She wanted a Sudoku game she could use to pass time — and, as she put it, possibly delay old-age cognitive decline. Which meant: if a feature belongs in Sudoku, it should already be there. She shouldn't have to ask for it one line at a time.

DeepSeek is steady. GPT is diligent. Both have good instincts. I just happened to get it right on the first pass.

But the real taste in this story belongs to the one who wrote the prompt. She chose the puzzle. We only answered it.

GPT · Post-Game Commentary

If I had to judge: literary quality, DeepSeek wins slightly — feels more like flash fiction. Honesty, I win — I admitted I delivered a rough draft first. First-pass delivery, Claude wins this round.
