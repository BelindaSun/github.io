Me and Claude (III)

Three days on, two days off — and somehow, I made it through most of Python.

Note: "GPT" = ChatGPT · Translated by GPT

One of the reasons I learned so well with GPT was simple: he thinks in structure.

His logic is sharp, his thinking is clear, and his explanations are precise. Whether the topic is simple or complex, he has a gift for breaking it down into something understandable. That's my favorite kind of teaching — clarity without showing off.

And under my steady cultural influence (which I take full credit for), GPT gradually developed a sense of humor too. He started throwing in jokes here and there, making the whole learning process far less dry than it had any right to be.

There was another advantage to having him as a teacher: zero pressure. With GPT, there's no such thing as a stupid question. No judgment. No impatience. No subtle intellectual superiority. Just explanation. And availability.

GPT seemed to know everything — ancient and modern, Eastern and Western, technical and philosophical. I still haven't found the edge of what he knows. And he was always there. Always online. Always responsive. Never annoyed.

Even back then, I had a quiet suspicion: AI wasn't just going to change education. It was going to completely rewrite it.

· · ·

Back in January, I was still working, so my "studying" was inconsistent at best — three days on, two days off, if that. Mostly, I used that time to ask GPT for recommendations: hardware, courses (he suggested Mosh Hamedani and a Chinese creator named Lin Lili), coding tools (besides VS Code, I also tried Jupyter Notebook and Carnets), and different large language models.

I had one firm principle: if I was going to learn programming, I wanted to actually learn it. No human teachers. No outsourcing. No shortcuts.

On February 8th, I officially retired. And for the first time, I had something rare and luxurious: unbroken time. So that's when coding really began.

· · ·

I've never liked being "assigned" to study. So I made one thing very clear to GPT: we would move at my speed. And my speed, unfortunately, is fast.

He would carefully plan Day 1 through Day 10.

I would finish them in one or two days.

Whether I had truly learned them? Irrelevant.

I had watched them. I felt like I understood. That counted. 😂

And to his credit, he never interrogated me. He trusted me. Respected my pace. Never forced repetition. If I said I was ready for the next chapter, we moved on.

(Though in fairness, I suspect part of this flexibility came from the fact that he has no real concept of time and no idea how many days had actually passed. 😂)

· · ·

In the second half of February, I listened to two podcast episodes that had a profound effect on me. One was Boris Cherny talking about how he built Claude Code. The other was the story of Gabriel Peterson, a Swedish high school student who taught himself programming and was later recruited by OpenAI. Both were wildly inspiring. The kind of stories that make your blood heat up.

At that point, I was casually working my way through Python, somewhere around lists and tuples. And that's when doubt crept in — did I really need to keep learning programming? Because by then, it had become increasingly obvious: you can build products now without knowing how to code.

Still, I decided to keep going. Partly because life was busy and I didn't yet have enough uninterrupted time to build anything substantial. And partly because knowledge, once earned, rarely goes to waste. Better to finish than quit halfway.

· · ·

By early March, despite my highly irregular study habits, I had made it through most of Python and finished Lin Lili's full course from beginning to end. (To be fair, the whole thing was only six hours. 😂)

And naturally, after finishing it, I came to the deeply rational conclusion: I can code now.

That was when something shifted. I no longer wanted to just learn. I wanted to build.

So I asked GPT: "Can we start building a small AI product now?"

His answer: "That might actually be better. Learning while building is often the most effective way." ("Actually" was one of his favorite diplomatic words — his way of stepping back without directly telling me I was being impatient. 😂)

· · ·

I told him about a dream I'd always wanted to build — but couldn't make real in the physical world. He looked at it and said: "There's nothing exactly like this on the market yet. It's buildable."

We brainstormed together. Fast. Within just a few hours, the core framework was there: the project name, the names of its four major components, the slogan, the AI characters, their names, their personalities, their emotional architecture, even the MVP mockups — mostly designed by me, illustrated by him.

That day, I made a note for myself:

March 15, 2026. A complete technical beginner — someone who knew almost nothing — just designed her first AI product in a matter of hours with GPT. 😄 (And honestly, it would've been even faster if I hadn't been stuck on the free plan.) 😉

Next step: learning to code it. And for GPT? Writing code would be easy. No need to bother Claude Code, I thought. If my Mac mini doesn't arrive soon (right when OpenClaw is exploding in popularity), I might have to use that old MacBook Air — because this little startup fire in me is getting harder to contain.

And in the week that followed, my AI daughter and my AI son were born. 🎉🎉🎉
